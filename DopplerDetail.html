Doppler Effect

The Doppler Effect explains how the observed frequency of a wave changes when a source or an observer moves. When the source travels toward you, waves bunch up to create a higher pitch. When it travels away, waves spread apart, lowering the pitch. This principle works for sound, light, and even ripples on water.

Relative motion Frequency shift Wave fronts

Approaching source

As a source of sound moves toward an observer, each wave crest is emitted from a position closer to the listener than the previous crest. This shortens the wavelength and raises the observed frequency.

  • Wave fronts bunch together in front of the source.
  • Pitch increases; light shifts toward the blue end of the spectrum.
  • Used to measure rotation speeds in astronomy.

Receding source

If the source moves away, each wave crest starts farther from the observer, stretching the wavelength and lowering the frequency that reaches the listener.

  • Wave fronts spread out behind the source.
  • Pitch decreases; light shifts toward the red end.
  • Explains why sirens drop in pitch as vehicles pass.

Real-world applications

The Doppler Effect powers technologies across science and medicine. Each relies on precise measurements of frequency changes to infer motion, distance, or speed.

  • Weather radar charts the speed of approaching storms.
  • Medical ultrasound tracks blood flow through arteries.
  • Astronomers detect exoplanets via tiny stellar wobbles.
  • Police radar guns estimate vehicle speeds instantly.
